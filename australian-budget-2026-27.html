Australian Federal Budget 2026–27

A budget in the shadow of war

A Middle East conflict has upended Australia's fiscal calculus. Surpluses are gone, inflation is surging and growth is slowing — yet the books are far stronger than almost any other rich country's.

Source: Australian Government Budget Papers 2026–27 and 2025–26 Budget night: 12 May 2026 Treasurer: Jim Chalmers
Middle East oil shock: A major conflict has disrupted roughly one-fifth of global seaborne oil and gas supply. Australian headline inflation surged to 4.6% in March 2026 (petrol +32.8% in a month) and is forecast to reach 5% by June 2026 before falling to 2½% by June 2027. GDP growth has been revised down from 2¼% to 1¾% for 2026–27.

Policy measures
What the budget does

The 2026–27 budget cuts taxes, funds hospitals and defence, reserves gas for domestic use and attempts structural reform of the NDIS.

$250
Working Australians Tax Offset
Annual tax offset for 13m+ workers from July 2027. Additional tax cuts in 2026 and 2027 for all taxpayers.
$25bn
Hospital funding
Additional investment in public hospitals under the National Health Reform Agreement, including $15.9bn pre-provisioned at MYEFO.
$53bn
Record defence
2026 National Defence Strategy and Integrated Investment Program. Defence expenses rise 18.8% over four years to 2029–30.
20%
Gas export reservation
One-fifth of gas exports reserved for domestic use. Fuel excise cut temporarily in response to fuel price surge from Middle East conflict.
65,000
New homes
Infrastructure funding to enable up to 65,000 new homes. Tax reform to help 75,000 more Australians become owner-occupiers over the decade.
$184.9bn
NDIS structural reform
Scheme growth reset to ~2% during transition, returning to 5% from 2030–31. Projected saving of $184.9bn over 12 years — the largest structural reform in this budget.
$10.2bn
Regulatory burden cut
Annual reduction in business regulatory costs. $3.5bn in new business tax measures; $540m/yr in compliance savings.
$8.6bn
Roads and rail
Significant road and rail infrastructure projects, plus housing infrastructure to support new construction activity.
Medium-term outlook
The path back to surplus — and how it changed

Two consecutive surpluses in 2022–23 and 2023–24 were the first back-to-back surpluses in nearly two decades. The oil shock has set back the return to balance.
