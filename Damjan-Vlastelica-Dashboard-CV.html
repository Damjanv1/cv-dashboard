My five favourite chart types
A lot of what I do is communicating analytical outcomes to stakeholders. These are five of my favourite charts for the job — in no particular order.
1.
Combination charts
Bar + line · dual axis · small multiples

Simplicity is usually better — but putting two measures side-by-side can provide essential context that neither tells on its own.

These charts were the workhorse of COVID reporting: daily case count (bar) layered with a cumulative curve (line). The second example inverts the pattern and adds small multiples to expose the interplay between case growth rate, test positivity, and daily testing volume across different parts of Sydney.

Chart Bar + Line · Source NSW Health
NSW COVID-19 cases — daily + cumulative
Case Growth Rate vs Test Positivity vs Daily Tests · by LGA grouping
Rest of Sydney
LGAs of Concern
Adjacent to LGAs of Concern
Case Growth Rate (PoP · 7-day avg) Test Positivity Rate # Daily Tests
2.
Box plots
Distributions at a glance · outlier detection

Box plots give you the key statistical information at a single glance — median, IQR, range, outliers — all without losing the shape of the distribution.

Broken out by a categorical field, they also yield insight into population differences. Here, move-accuracy for my chess.com games (scored by Stockfish) is split by game phase — opening / midgame / endgame — and by outcome. The midgame tells most of the story: in losses, my midgame accuracy collapses.

Chart Box-and-whisker · Source chess.com · Stockfish eval
Chess move accuracy, by phase of game (wins vs losses)
3.
Sankey charts
Path dependence · flow decomposition

Sankeys can be a dog's breakfast when done poorly — but done well, they're excellent for telling the story of path dependence: how one outcome leads to another.

Same chess data, different question: does winning the opening translate to winning the game? I wrote a case statement comparing my opening / midgame / endgame accuracy against my opponent's — WO = won opening, LO = lost opening, with the same logic for WM/LM and WE/LE in the granular view on the right.

Chart Sankey · Source chess.com
Opening result → Game result
Opening → full (opening · midgame · endgame) path
4.
Funnel charts
Conversion · distribution · age-outcome skew

Funnels aren't just for conversion. Sorting by pre-binned age brackets, they neatly visualise how outcome severity shifts between populations.

Here, the NSW 2021 winter outbreak shows the classic COVID skew: young people dominate case counts, but hospitalisation and ICU concentrate in the 30–69 bracket — a textbook example of the same denominator producing three very different shapes.

Chart Funnel · Source NSW Health (21 Aug 2021)
Age distribution · Cases / Hospitalisation / ICU — NSW '21 Winter Outbreak
5.
The (humble) bar chart
Sort · colour · reveal · don't overthink it

Often the simplest way to illustrate an insight — and extended considerably with a thoughtful sort and a second categorical encoding via colour.

Bar charts are the charts I find myself using the most, switching between vertical and horizontal to add variation. The example here is NBA teams sorted by arena capacity, coloured by conference — revealing that the highest-capacity arenas sit in the Eastern markets.

A quick word on data storytelling
“Data storyteller” gets thrown around as a desirable skill — to the point where it's been promoted to a role itself. A full treatment is beyond this document, but there is one technique I've used repeatedly with non-technical audiences that is genuinely underutilised: the data build.
◆
Data builds · one chart, three views
Take stakeholders through the insight · don't drop them in at the end

A data build uses two or more views of the same chart to walk an audience through an insight. You use different views of the same canvas to build the story, making sure the outcome is clearly understood rather than asserted.

Good data storytelling should reveal patterns without bias, provide context & articulate insight, streamline cognition and hold engagement. Here's the same scatter — 3PT FG% vs 3PT attempts — used three ways.

1
Start with the raw chart. It already carries a plethora of information — only a few shooters attempt 3s at high volume, % decreases marginally with volume, a handful sustain efficiency at high volume, some outlier high-% shooters live at low volume.
3PT FG% vs 3PT attempted · Top shooters only
2
Add context. A 5-year league-average baseline (horizontal rule) and a reference volume (vertical rule) turn the same data into something decision-ready — one can now see how many top shooters sit above the modern-era NBA average, and at what volume.
…with league-average reference
3
Highlight exemplars & transition. Name the outliers, colour the hero (Stephen Curry). The chart becomes a bridge to a related story — place in NBA history, evolution of three-point shooting, positional archetype.
…with exemplary cases highlighted

When generating insights I collect every one I believe has business value, run rough commercial modelling on each to give it a directional $ value, order by that value, then tell a 3–8 slide data story for each using data builds and related techniques.
